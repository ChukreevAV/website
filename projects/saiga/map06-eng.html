The aim of this page is to provide detailed description for the newly created map "Saiga antelope historic and current distribution (Russian case study)". This resource was developed in order to give thorough explanations on methodology implemented and, additionally with the objective to acknowledge the initial sources of all the types of the data (graphics, population data, etc.), that is difficult to do on the map itself.

Subject of the map

Saiga tatarica tatarica Linnaeus, 1766 (Bannikov 1989), its prehistoric, historical and recent ranges with emphasise on the recent dynamics of Russian part of the population.

Map structure

The map includes one central map with two insets. These three insets are logically linked together, each representing the enlarged part of previous one. The border colors and numbers in circles ensure the readability of maps and depict order in which end-user can visualize them.

Map 1 is an inset map and it's showing prehistoric finds during Pleistocene. Pleistocene warming and accompanying vegetation change limited the Saiga range to deserts and grasslands of Central Asia, Mongolia and China, this distribution is shown as the historic range of the species. Map 1 is also showing a boundary of the Map 2 and related number of this map.

Map 2 is an inset map and it's showing current population ranges. Today, distribution of Saiga is limited to the several islands populations, seldom connected by occasional migrations. Saiga can be found in Russia, Kazakhstan and Mongolia now. Map 2 is also showing a boundary of the Map 3 and related number of this map.

Map 3 is the main component of the map, showing dynamics of the Saiga ranges in Republic of Kalmykia of Russian Federation from 50s till middle of 90s.

Topics covered

Following topics are shown on the map, after the topic (the name of it is shown in bold) see references which lead to exact publication from which data was taken:

  • Prehistoric founds [1, 2]
  • Historic range [1, 2]
    Based on the analysis of F. Koppen in 1891 [7], C. Greve in 1898 [8], that presented most complete survey of the distribution of the saiga in various periods of history. T. Adolf in 1948 [9] added data for Asiatic part of the range. S. Kirikov (1959) has carried out complex analysis of historical and ancient documents and presented new information on distribution of saiga in the past, this was combined with firsthand data by A. Bannikov (1958, 1961) [1, 2]
  • Current population ranges [3, 4]
    Approximate ranges of modern populations, Russian population range is shown after [4]
  • Distribution in Russia (mainly Republic of Kalmykia and Astrakhan Region) in 1950-65 [4]
  • Distribution in Russia (mainly Republic of Kalmykia and Astrakhan Region) in 1988-94 [4]
    For both ranges, permanently inhabited area shown, taking into account seasonal movement. On periphery, substantial buffer zone of occasional dispersals. Boundaries of this buffer are not constant and Permanently inhabited area is more stable.
  • Lambing grounds in Russia in 1957-61 [4]
  • Lambing grounds in Russia in 1988-94 [4]
  • Population dynamics for Russian population of saiga since 1976 till 2001 [3]

  • Drawings of A.N. Komarov (Running saiga couple, taken from [5]) and V. Smirin (Feeding and Saiga family, taken from [1, 6]).
  • Basemap is Vmap0 vector dataset produced by NIMA (now NGA) in 2000 [11] (more information, in Russian) and SRTM digital raster elevation dataset from NASA [12] (more information, in Russian). The relief is slightly exagerrated to show variations between relatively flat and hilly terrains. Generally the area is relatively low undulated.
  • Photo of young saiga male by Pavel Sorokin [13].
